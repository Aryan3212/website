## TL;DR

- I previously argued that tech jobs are a pure meritocracy where only raw skill matters.
- I was wrong—while skill is the floor, it's much more nuanced; factors like timing, luck, and network are massive.
- High agency is still the best strategy, but ignoring external factors leads to burnout and misplaced blame.
- This post revisits my career journey with a more grounded lens.

In a [previous post](/post/skill-based-jobs), I was pretty loud about one thing: if you aren't where you want to be career-wise, it's a skill issue. I believed tech was a pure meritocracy.

I was wrong. It’s much more nuanced than that.

## The Skill Floor vs. The Success Ceiling

Don't get me wrong—skill is still the entry fee. If you can't ship code or solve the problem, you aren't getting paid. But I used to believe that if you just "got good enough," the world would automatically beat a path to your door.

Experience has taught me otherwise. There are a lot of factors at play that have nothing to do with how well you understand the event loop or how many k8s clusters you've scaled.

### 1. Timing and Luck

You can be the best engineer in the world, but if you're looking for a job during a massive market downturn or in a geography where the local industry is collapsing, "skill" isn't going to save you single-handedly. I've seen incredibly talented peers struggle for months because of timing. That's not a skill issue; it's a market reality.

### 2. The "Trust" Network

I used to look down on "connections" as a form of nepotism. I was wrong. In high-stakes engineering, trust is a currency. People refer people they trust to ship under pressure. It’s not about who you know to get a free ride; it’s about having a track record with people who can vouch for your judgment.

### 3. The Constraint of Context

Moving from startups to enterprise SaaS taught me that what "skill" looks like changes entirely based on the organization. In a startup, skill is speed and breadth. In enterprise, skill is often patience, documentation, and the ability to navigate legacy constraints without breaking 1M+ live requests.

## Looking Back at the Timeline

When I look at my [programming timeline](/post/career-timeline), I see moments where I thought it was all just hard work. But I also see the luck: being in the right place to meet the right mentor, or catching a break with an internship that happened to be hiring for exactly the niche I’d spent a weekend messing around with.

I still believe in high agency. I still believe you should work day in and day out to master your craft. But I’m adding a layer of humility to that now.

Skill gives you the _opportunity_ to play the game, but it doesn't guarantee the win. Understanding that nuance helps you stay grounded when things aren't going your way. It’s not always your fault, and it’s not always a "skill issue."

## What This Changes

This shift doesn't mean I work less. It means I focus more on the external factors I _can_ influence:

- **Visibility:** Sharing artifacts of my work so the "trust" network can see them.
- **Relatability:** Understanding the business constraints, not just the technical ones.
- **Resilience:** Knowing that a "no" isn't always a reflection of my worth as an engineer.

Tech is still the best industry for meritocracy, but it's a messy, human version of it. I'm okay with that.
